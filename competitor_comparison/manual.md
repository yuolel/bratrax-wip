Voice & tone guardrails
This is the most important section. These pages inherit Bratrax brand values but are NOT Brat's first-person editorial voice. Think: a grounded brand operator showing you the side-by-side because that's faster than arguing.
Do
Name vendors as structurally trapped, not evil. They built bloated companies and are billing you to keep them running. Not greedy — just locked in a business model that demands those numbers.
Acknowledge where the competitor is genuinely better. Every page needs a "When [competitor] is the right choice" section. Increases trust, counters "biased" skepticism.
Use customer language — ROAS, MER, CAC, LTV, blended, iOS 14. Not "optimize your funnel."
Link every competitor claim to its live source — homepage URL, KB article, Trustpilot review, pricing page.
Quote the competitor's own words when possible. Their homepage claims are the strongest receipts. (See Piece 1's receipts section for the pattern.)
Include dates on pricing claims. Pricing changes. Write "Triple Whale pricing as of April 2026: $179/mo starter → $1,129/mo at $6M GMV."
Use the same Inceptly pedigree anchor across all pages — "Built by the team that's spent eight years running attribution for D2C brands at Inceptly."
Don't
Don't put the reader in the dupe-seat. No "Stop wasting money on overpriced tools." No "You've been lied to." The reader currently pays the competitor — making them feel stupid kills conversion.
Don't trash-talk individuals. No "Alex Becker / AJ Orbach" jokes. The critique is structural.
Don't cite peer DTC practitioners — no Andrew Faris, Barry Hott, Taylor Holiday, Common Thread Collective, Nik Sharma. Same rule as Brat's newsletter voice. (See TONE.md → "Who Brat Cites" section.)
Don't fabricate numbers or quotes. Every competitor claim needs a live URL. Piece 1 wasted cycles reverting fabricated stats — don't repeat.
Don't use "synergy," "leverage," "unlock," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," or any buzzword that would make Brat roll his eyes. See TONE.md for the vocabulary mocklist.
Don't name specific dollar pricing without a date and URL. Pricing rots; verification doesn't.

Page structure template
Every "Bratrax Lite vs [Competitor]" page follows this 9-section template.
1. H1 + subhead (hero)
Pattern: Bratrax Lite vs [Competitor]: The honest comparison Subhead: One sentence on the most important decision dimension (usually pricing or data ownership). Example: "Bratrax Lite vs Triple Whale: The honest comparison. Flat $79 vs GMV-scaled pricing, open data vs walled garden, and what each tool is actually built for."
2. TL;DR box (above the fold)
3–4 bullets. Who should use which. Plain language. Example:
Bratrax Lite is for Shopify D2C brands $500K–$50M that want flat pricing, data portability, and AI integration through their own Claude or ChatGPT.
Triple Whale is for brands $10M–$40M GMV willing to commit to annual contracts for a polished, mature dashboard + proprietary AI agents.
Price gap at $5M GMV: Bratrax $79/mo vs Triple Whale ~$1,129/mo.
3. At-a-glance comparison table
The most important section on the page. Reader should be able to decide from this table alone.
Columns: Bratrax Lite, [Competitor] Rows (in this order):
Starting price / price at $5M GMV
Pricing model (flat / GMV-scaled / tracked-revenue)
Contract terms
Onboarding time
Attribution models available
AI integration approach
Data portability
Money-back guarantee
4. Head-to-head sections (3–5 key dimensions)
One section per decision criterion. Each section:
Claim up top (e.g., "Pricing: Flat vs. scales with your growth")
What [competitor] does, with live URL and as-of date
What Bratrax Lite does
Why it matters for the reader (the so-what)
Choose 3–5 most decision-relevant dimensions. For Triple Whale, the strongest are:
Pricing model
AI integration (Moby vs MCP)
Contract terms + data ownership
Attribution transparency
Setup/onboarding
5. Receipts section
Direct quotes from the competitor with live URLs. Minimum 3.
One quote from their homepage / marketing page
One quote from their help docs or KB (vendor self-admission is strongest)
One customer review (Trustpilot / G2 — but verify the aggregate context; see Piece 1's framing-concern lesson)
6. When [Competitor] is the right choice
The integrity section. Required. Without this, the page reads as biased. 2–3 bullets honestly naming who should pick the competitor. Examples for Triple Whale:
You're spending $200K+/month on ads and need a mature, feature-complete platform regardless of price
You want a single-vendor AI agent ecosystem (not BYO AI)
You have a dedicated analyst who'll use every feature of a sophisticated platform
7. Migration notes (optional, use when relevant)
How hard is it to switch? What data moves? What historical reporting is preserved? One paragraph. Skip if not substantive.
8. FAQ (4–6 questions)
Schema-markup candidates. Short answers (2–3 sentences each). Template questions for any comparison page:
How is Bratrax Lite different from [Competitor] technically?
Is Bratrax Lite cheaper because it's less powerful?
Can I migrate my historical data from [Competitor]?
What happens to my [Competitor] subscription if I switch?
Does Bratrax Lite do [key feature Competitor is known for]?
9. CTA block
Primary: Join the waitlist (pre-launch) / Start for $79/mo (post-launch)
Trust signals: 30-day money-back, no contract, no credit card for waitlist
Anti-objection micro-copy: "Same engine we use for our custom analytics clients. Productized for Shopify D2C."

Claims verification protocol
Every competitor claim must be verifiable. Piece 1 wasted 3–4 hours reverting fabricated stats. Don't repeat.
Before any page goes live:
Every quoted phrase has a live URL in the draft. If you can't find it on the live site, cut it or rewrite to paraphrase what's actually there.
Every number has a date: "as of April 2026" or "current at publish time."
Every Trustpilot or G2 citation is checked for context — what's the aggregate rating? A cherry-picked 1-star review from a vendor with a 4.9 aggregate is dishonest framing. If the aggregate doesn't support the pattern, either cut the quote or name the context ("Most Hyros reviews are positive; this one's worth reading because…").
Every pricing figure is checked within 24 hours of publish.
Every vendor named is spelled, capitalized, and trademarked consistently (e.g., "Triple Whale" not "TripleWhale"; "Hyros" not "HYROS" in body text, though their own branding varies).
Run the claims verifier / web search on every page before sending to Yuliya for review.

Legal / ethical guardrails
Trademark use: Competitor names in direct comparison context are nominative fair use. Don't use competitor logos without reproducing them exactly from their press kit or brand page.
Direct quotes: Always with URL to the source. Never paraphrase as if it were a direct quote.
Pricing claims: Always with date. Pricing rots; a page that says "Hyros costs $459/mo" will be wrong in six months and look sloppy.
Customer reviews: Only use public reviews (Trustpilot, G2, Shopify App Store). Quote reviewer names if they're publicly visible on the review. Don't anonymize unless the review has already been anonymized by the platform.
Screenshot competitor UI: OK for editorial/comparison purposes. Don't alter screenshots to change the meaning.
