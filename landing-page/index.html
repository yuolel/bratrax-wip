01 — The Problem

You bought the tool.
You hired the person.
You're still going with your gut.

Hyros. Triple Whale. GA4. Maybe a data hire. The numbers still don't match. You're making half-million-dollar bets on gut feel.

The problem isn't the data. It's that Marketing, Finance, and your CEO all use the same word — “conversion” — and mean three completely different things.

Marketing says
“Conversion”
Someone submitted a form. Lead captured. Pipeline moving. ROAS looks great.
Finance says
“Conversion”
Payment cleared. Revenue recognized. Money in the account. ROAS looks terrible.
The CEO hears
“Conversion”
Both versions. No way to tell who's right. Can't make the call to scale or cut.

Everyone's right. Everyone's using different definitions. And you've been making decisions based on a word that meant three different things to the people who voted on it. That's the problem no dashboard can solve.

03 — The Step Every Vendor Skips

Your numbers don't match because
your team never agreed on
what the words mean.

Every analytics tool starts with the data. We start with the people. Before we write a line of code, we fix the definitions.

{ }
Step 1

Name the things

What are the nouns in your business? Users, orders, campaigns, subscriptions. What do those words actually mean to each department? You'd be surprised how often nobody agrees.

[ ]
Step 2

Define what matters

What does “active” mean? When does a “lead” become a “customer”? We get every department in a room and don't leave until the definitions are shared — not assumed.

</>
Step 3

Map the connections

How do things relate? A user has subscriptions. A campaign belongs to a brand. These connections are your business logic — made visible, made explicit, and agreed upon.

The result is an ontology — a shared dictionary that every dashboard, report, and metric inherits from. It's the reason your team stops arguing about definitions and starts arguing about strategy. That's the upgrade.
