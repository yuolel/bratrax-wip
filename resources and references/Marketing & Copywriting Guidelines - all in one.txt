The $100M Leads Manual for Marketers & Copywriters

This manual summarizes the key strategies and principles from Alex Hormozi's "$100M Leads" to help you, as a marketer or copywriter, understand how to get strangers to want to buy your stuff through effective lead generation.

Section 1: Understanding the Basics

What is a Lead?

A lead is someone who has a problem you can solve and the money to spend. However, not all leads are created equal.

The Importance of Engaged Leads

The true goal of advertising isn't just to get any contact information; it's to get engaged leads – people who show genuine interest in what you sell. This means they might:

* Give their contact information on your website.
* Follow you on social media and allow contact.
* Reply to your email campaigns.

Focus on attracting people who are actually interested in your offer, not just those who randomly click an ad.

The Foundation: A Grand Slam Offer

"$100M Leads" builds upon the concepts in Hormozi's first book, "$100M Offers". It assumes you already have a Grand Slam Offer – something so good that people feel stupid saying no. You need a compelling product or service to effectively generate and convert leads.

Why Focus on Leads?

* It's the first step to getting more customers.
* More leads make it hard to be poor.
* Advertising, which generates leads, gives you endless chances to get it right in business.

Section 2: The Core Four Ways to Get Leads

There are only four fundamental ways to let people know about what you sell:

1. Warm Outreach: Reaching out directly to people you already know (your warm audience). This is one-to-one communication with a warm audience.
  * Examples: Calling friends about your new service, sending a personalized email to past colleagues about your lead magnet.
2. Posting Free Content: Creating valuable, free content that attracts your ideal audience. This is one-to-many communication with a warm audience (as they become familiar with your content).
  * Examples: Sharing helpful tips on social media, writing blog posts solving common problems your target audience faces.
3. Cold Outreach: Reaching out directly to people you don't know (your cold audience). This is one-to-one communication with a cold audience.
  * Examples: Sending targeted emails to businesses that fit your ideal customer profile, direct messaging potential clients on professional networking sites.
4. Running Paid Ads: Paying to show your message to a large group of people you don't know. This is one-to-many communication with a cold audience.
  * Examples: Running Facebook ads targeting specific demographics interested in your niche, using Google Ads to appear when people search for relevant keywords.

Mastering these four methods is crucial for consistent lead generation.

Section 3: Engaging Your Leads with a Powerful Lead Magnet

A lead magnet is a valuable free offering you provide in exchange for a prospect's contact information, turning them into an engaged lead. Think of it as an ethical bribe.

Seven Steps to Creating an Effective Lead Magnet:

1. Figure out the problem you want to solve and who to solve it for.
  * Example: This book itself is a lead magnet aiming to solve the "not getting enough leads" problem for entrepreneurs making under $1,000,000 in annual profit.
2. Figure out how to solve it. What valuable information or tool can you provide?
3. Figure out how to deliver it. Consider different formats:
  * Software: A free tool, template, or calculator.
  * Information: Ebooks, guides, checklists, case studies.
    * Example: A free checklist of "10 Ways to Improve Your Website Conversion Rates."
  * Services: A free consultation, audit, or trial.
    * Example: A free 30-minute website SEO audit.
  * Physical Products: A sample, assessment chart, or useful item.
4. Test what to name it. The headline matters!
  * Example: Testing "Get More Website Leads" vs. "How to Get Strangers to Want to Buy Your Stuff". "How to" often performs better.
5. Make it easy to consume. Offer it in multiple formats (video, audio, text) and make it accessible on different devices.
  * Example: Offering an ebook as a PDF, EPUB, and audiobook.
6. Make it darn good. Provide so much value that people feel obligated to eventually pay you. Give away the secrets, sell the implementation.
  * Example: Providing a detailed guide with actionable steps, not just surface-level information.
7. Make it easy for them to tell you they want more (Call To Action - CTA). Tell them what to do next and give them a reason to do it now.
  * Example: Include a clear button or link to "Book a Free Strategy Session" or "Learn More About Our Paid Program."

Remember: A Grand Slam Offer works for free stuff (lead magnets) just as well as it does for paid stuff.

Section 4: Implementing the Core Four - Strategies and Examples

Warm Outreach: Nurturing Your Network

* Focus on providing value first. Offer your lead magnet or a free consultation.
* Ask for referrals. Don't just ask them to buy; ask if they know anyone who might benefit from your offer.
  * Example Script: "I'm helping [ideal customer] get [dream outcome] in [time period] without [effort and sacrifice]. Do you know anyone who might be interested?"
* Make it easy to say yes. Start with a free offer.
* Treat your warm list as a valuable, growing asset. Consistent outreach can lead to significant revenue.
* Benchmark: Aim for about one in five warm contacts to engage.

Posting Free Content: Building Your Audience

* Content Unit: Hook, Retain, Reward. Every piece of content should grab attention (Hook), keep them engaged (Retain), and deliver on the promise (Reward).
  * Hook Examples:
    * Call Outs: "Attention Small Business Owners!"
    * Questions: "Struggling to Get Leads?"
    * Bold Statements: "Double Your Website Traffic in 30 Days!"
  * Retain Examples:
    * Lists: "5 Reasons Why Your Ads Aren't Converting"
    * Steps: "3 Steps to Writing a Compelling Headline"
    * Stories: Sharing a client success story related to lead generation.
  * Reward Example: If your hook promised "3 Steps to Writing a Compelling Headline," your content must clearly explain those three steps.
* Master the Give : Ask Ratio. Provide a lot of value before asking for something in return (e.g., sign-up for your lead magnet or purchase your offer).
* Monetize strategically. Use integrated offers within your content or intermittent offers (occasional promotions among valuable content).
  * Lead Magnet Example: After sharing lead generation tips, offer a free checklist of "11 More Lead Generation Strategies".
  * Offer Example: Occasionally directly promote your core service within your content.
* Scale your content creation. Start on one platform and gradually expand to others, repurposing content where possible.
* Talk about your experiences ("How I" instead of "How to") to build trust.
* Repeat your message. People need reminders.

Cold Outreach: Reaching Strangers Effectively

* Be specific with your targeting. Identify your ideal customer precisely.
* Build targeted lead lists. Use software, hire brokers, or manually compile lists.
* Provide the biggest, fastest value you can afford with your lead magnet or offer.
* Write compelling scripts for phone, email, and direct messages. Keep them concise.
* Automate delivery and distribution where ethical and possible.
* Follow up multiple times in multiple ways. Timing is crucial.
* Benchmark: Aim for around 3% of your email list to turn into engaged leads.

Running Paid Ads: Scaling with Money

* Target precisely. Select platforms where your ideal audience spends time and use targeting options (lookalike audiences, interests, demographics).
* Craft your ad with three key elements: Call Out + Value + Call to Action (CTA).
  * Call Out: Grab the attention of your ideal customer using labels, questions, or "if-then" statements.
    * Example: "Attention Chiropractors struggling to get new patients!"
  * Value: Clearly explain the benefits of your lead magnet or offer using the "What-Who-When" framework. Explain what you offer, who it's for, and when they'll see results.
  * Call to Action (CTA): Tell them exactly what to do next (click a button, visit a website, call a number) and make it easy.
    * Example: "Click the 'Learn More' button below to download your free guide."
* Get permission to contact them by offering your valuable lead magnet in exchange for their information.
* Focus on efficiency (Return on Investment - ROI).
* Start with a small, specific audience and scale as you become profitable.
* Benchmark: Aim for a strong return on your ad spend.

Section 5: Scaling Your Lead Generation with "Lead Getters"

Once you have a solid foundation, you can leverage others to get leads for you:

* Customer Referrals: Make your product so good that customers naturally refer others. Actively ask for referrals and incentivize them.
  * Example: Offering a discount or bonus to customers who refer new clients.
* Employees: Train your team to actively participate in lead generation using the Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate method.
  * Document: Create checklists of your lead-getting processes.
  * Demonstrate: Show your employees how to execute these processes.
  * Duplicate: Have them perform the tasks while you observe and provide feedback.
* Agencies: Partner with marketing or sales agencies to drive leads for your business.
* Affiliates and Partners: Collaborate with other businesses or individuals who have an audience similar to yours. Offer them a commission for every lead or customer they send your way.
  * Example: A fitness coach promoting your nutrition supplements to their clients.

Section 6: Putting It All Together - Your Advertising Plan

* Pick a lead type to focus on: Customers, Affiliates, Employees, or Agencies.
* Commit to consistent daily advertising actions: Follow the Rule of 100 (100 primary actions per day for 100 days) or work "open to goal" (dedicate a specific time block until you reach your daily goal).
  * Example (Rule of 100 for Warm Outreach): Send 100 personalized emails or direct messages to your network each day for 100 days.
* Use a One Page Advertising Checklist to track your daily actions.
* Do this consistently until you can afford to pay someone else to do it. Then, reinvest your time in higher-level strategic activities or training new team members.

Conclusion: The Path to $100M Leads

Getting a consistent flow of engaged leads is the lifeblood of a successful business. By understanding the core four advertising methods, crafting compelling lead magnets, and strategically scaling your efforts through lead getters, you can build your own "$100M Leads Machine". The key is consistent effort, continuous learning, and a commitment to providing value. Remember, you cannot lose if you do not quit. Start with one method, master it, and then expand. Good luck!
The Marketer's Guide to Crafting $100M Offers: A Summary of Alex Hormozi's Principles

This manual provides a clear and concise overview of the key strategies and principles outlined in Alex Hormozi's "$100M Offers," aimed at helping marketers and copywriters understand how to create offers so compelling that people feel foolish saying no.

Section 1: Understanding the Foundation - The Grand Slam Offer

* The Offer is Everything: The starting point of any successful business transaction is the offer – what you provide in exchange for money.
* What is a Grand Slam Offer? It's more than just a product or service. It's a combination of pricing, value, guarantees, and naming strategies designed to reliably turn advertising dollars into significant profits. It's an offer so good, your prospects feel stupid saying no.
* The Goal: To create an offer that cannot be easily compared to competitors, allowing you to sell in a "category of one". This shifts the purchasing decision from a price-driven one to a value-driven one.
* Example: Instead of just offering "marketing services," a Grand Slam Offer might be "Guaranteed 20 Leads in Your First Month or Your Next Month is Free + Our Complete Sales Playbook for Your Industry."

Section 2: The Problem with Being a Commodity - Why Price Isn't Everything

* The Commodity Trap: A commodity is a product or service available from many places. When your offer is seen as a commodity, customers primarily base their decision on price.
* The Race to the Bottom: Competing on price leads to thin profit margins, making it difficult to grow and invest in your business.
* Grand Slam Offers as the Solution: By creating a differentiated and high-value offer, you escape the price war and establish yourself as unique.
* Example: Two gyms offer similar monthly memberships. One is a commodity. The other offers a "Transformation Triumph Bundle" including personalized meal plans, workout guides, and weekly coaching calls for a slightly higher price. The second is harder to compare on price alone.

Section 3: Pricing for Profit - Charging What You're Worth

* Focus on Value, Not Just Price: People buy when they believe the value they receive is greater than the price they pay.
* Lowering Price is Usually the Wrong Move: It's a short-term solution that erodes profitability. Instead, focus on increasing the perceived value.
* The Virtuous Cycle of Price: Charging premium prices allows you to:
  * Invest more in your product/service and customer experience.
  * Attract better clients who are easier to satisfy.
  * Multiply your profit margins.
* Premium Pricing is a Moral Imperative: By charging more, you can deliver more value and get your customers more invested in achieving results.
* Example: A freelancer charges $50/hour for writing. By niching down and offering a "B2B SaaS Conversion Copywriting System with a Performance Guarantee" at a premium price, they are charging for the value of increased sales, not just their time.

Section 4: Creating Irresistible Value - The Value Equation and Offer Construction

* The Value Equation: Value = (Dream Outcome * Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay * Effort & Sacrifice).
  * Increase: the prospect's desired result and their belief that they will achieve it with your offer.
  * Decrease: the time it takes to achieve the result and the effort/sacrifice required from the prospect.
* Focus on the Bottom of the Equation: While promising big results is easy, the best companies focus on making things faster, easier, and more effortless for their customers.
* The 5 Steps to Creating a Grand Slam Offer:
  1. Identify the Dream Outcome: What specific result does your ideal customer deeply desire?
    * Example: Not just "lose weight," but "fit into their favorite jeans from 10 years ago with more energy."
  2. List All Problems: What obstacles and limiting beliefs prevent your ideal customer from achieving their dream outcome? Think in detail about the before and after of using your product/service.
    * Example (for the "lose weight" outcome): Confusing diets, lack of time to cook, not knowing what exercises to do, feeling hungry, social pressure, lack of motivation.
  3. Define Solutions: Turn each problem into a specific solution your offer will provide. Frame them in a benefit-oriented way.
    * Example:
      * Problem: Confusing diets.
      * Solution: Easy-to-follow meal plans with simple recipes.
      * Solution Wording: How to make buying healthy food easy and enjoyable, so that anyone can do it.
  4. Create Your Solutions Delivery Vehicles ("The How"): Brainstorm all the ways you could deliver each solution. Consider different levels of personal attention (one-on-one, group, self-study) and effort required from the client (done-for-you, do-it-with-you, do-it-yourself).
    * Example (for "easy-to-follow meal plans"): Downloadable PDF meal plans, video cooking demonstrations, a mobile app with recipes, live weekly Q&A about meal prep.
  5. Trim & Stack:
    * Trim: Remove delivery methods that are high cost and low value to you.
    * Stack: Combine the remaining high-value, low-cost delivery methods into a comprehensive bundle. Give each component a benefit-driven name.
    * Example: Instead of selling each meal plan, workout guide, and coaching call separately, bundle them into the "Ultimate Transformation System" with specific names for each part (e.g., "5-Minute Fat-Burning Morning Routine," "Foolproof Bargain Grocery System").

Section 5: Enhancing Your Offer - The Power of Psychological Levers

Once you have a valuable core offer, you can make it even more irresistible using these psychological triggers:

* Scarcity: Limit the quantity of your offer to increase its perceived value and drive faster purchasing decisions.
  * Types: Limited number of spots, limited-time releases of products.
  * Example: "Only 10 seats left for our exclusive workshop!" "This limited-edition flavor is only available while supplies last!"
* Urgency: Create a time-sensitive reason for prospects to act now.
  * Types: Enrollment deadlines, limited-time discounts or bonuses.
  * Example: "Enroll by Friday to get our fast-action bonus worth $297!" "Price increasing next week - lock in your savings now!"
* Bonuses: Add extra value on top of your core offer to make it a no-brainer.
  * Key Principles: Make them relevant to the core offer, give them benefit-driven names, assign them a value, and ideally, they should address specific concerns of the prospect. Offer bonuses after asking for the sale.
  * Example: "Sign up today and get our exclusive 'Plateau-Busting Secrets' guide (valued at $97) absolutely free!"
* Guarantees: Reverse the risk for your prospects and build trust.
  * Types: Unconditional ("No questions asked" refund), Conditional (tied to the prospect taking specific actions), "Anti-Guarantee" (for highly exclusive offers). Conditional guarantees that promise a specific outcome are often the most powerful.
  * Example (Conditional): "If you follow our meal plan and workout program for 90 days and don't see results, we'll give you a full refund and an additional month of coaching for free!"
* Naming: Craft compelling and benefit-driven names for your offer and its components to grab attention and clearly communicate value to your ideal customer (your "avatar"). Use the M-A-G-I-C formula:
  * Magnet (Reason Why): Something that grabs attention (e.g., "Free," "Instant," "Guaranteed").
  * Avatar: Clearly identifies the target audience (e.g., "For Busy Moms," "For SaaS Founders").
  * Goal: States the desired outcome (e.g., "Lose 10lbs," "Generate 100 Leads").
  * Interval: Specifies the timeframe for achieving the goal (e.g., "In 30 Days," "In 6 Weeks").
  * Container: Uses a word that suggests a complete system or program (e.g., "Challenge," "Blueprint," "System").
  * Example: Instead of "Weight Loss Program," try "Free 6-Week Fat Loss Challenge for Busy Professionals."

Section 6: Making it Happen - Your First $100,000

* Focus on Implementation: Reading and understanding is just the first step. The real results come from taking action and implementing these strategies.
* Start with a Grand Slam Offer: This is the foundational building block for a successful business.
* Don't Be Afraid to Charge What You're Worth: Profit is essential for growth and allows you to serve more people.
* Iterate and Improve: Your first offer might not be perfect. Continuously test, gather feedback, and refine your offer based on what works.

By understanding and applying these principles, even novice marketers and copywriters can begin crafting offers that stand out, resonate with their target audience, and ultimately drive significant revenue. Remember, the key is to focus on creating immense value and communicating it effectively.
🧠 Breakthrough Advertising: A Beginner’s Guide to Persuasive Copy

Based on the principles from the book "Breakthrough Advertising" by Eugene M. Schwartz

PART 1: LAYING THE FOUNDATION OF PERSUASION

🔥 1. Tap into Existing Mass Desire

You don't create desire—you identify and channel existing ones (like the desire for safety, self-improvement, status).

Examples:

* A dating app doesn’t invent the desire for love—it channels it.
* A luxury car channels desire for prestige and recognition.
* An anti-aging cream taps into the fear of growing old.

How to Use:

* Identify the most relevant desire based on urgency, staying power, and scale.
* Analyze your product deeply (features, emotional benefits, USP).
* Focus that desire directly onto your product.



🧠 2. Understand Your Prospect’s State of Awareness

Schwartz outlined five awareness levels. Tailor your copy to match where your audience is:

Column 1	Column 2	Column 3
Awareness Level	Focus On	Headline Example
Most Aware	A deal or offer	“Save 20% on [Product] Today!”
Product Aware	A key benefit	“[Product] Makes [Benefit] Easier”
Solution Aware	Their desire or solution	“Finally! A Way to [Do X] Without [Y]”
Problem Aware	Their pain	“Tired of Back Pain? Here’s the Fix”
Completely Unaware	A hidden dream or fear	“They Laughed When I Sat at the Piano…”




🧩 3. Understand Market Sophistication

Markets evolve. Your copy should reflect how “saturated” the category is:

Stage	What To Do	Example
1	Make a direct claim	“Relieves Pain Fast”
2	Add specifics + proof	“Back Pain Gone in 15 Minutes - Guaranteed”
3	Introduce a unique mechanism	“Revolutionary Tech Targets Pain Instantly”
4	Improve your mechanism	“Now with Advanced Dual-Relief Formula”
5	Indirect/identity-based approach	“To Those Who Struggle with Sleepless Nights…”




✍️ 4. Craft Powerful Headlines

Your headline’s job: get the first sentence read.

Tactics:

* Ask a question: “Who Else Wants a Whiter Smile?”
* Offer how-to: “How to Sleep Through the Night Naturally”
* Use authority: “What Doctors Do When They Feel Sick”
* Highlight newness: “Now! A Car That Runs Without Gas”
* Challenge belief: “You’re Twice as Smart as You Think”

❌ Avoid lazy headline swaps. Base headlines on real insight into the market + product.



PART 2: MASTER THE 7 COPY TECHNIQUES

1. 🎯 Intensification

Amplify their existing desire. Paint a vivid, emotional picture of life after your product.

Example: “Feel the surge as you accelerate, leaving the city behind... Imagine heads turning.”

Thirteen ways to intensify desire:

Clear claims, vivid language, personalize (“you”), future pacing, comparisons, expert backing, analogies, stretch benefits over time, testimonials, guarantees, and more.


2. 👤 Identification

Make your product part of the reader’s identity.

Example: A Rolex isn’t just a watch—it’s proof of success.

Ask: What does your audience aspire to be? Position your product as a way to become that.



3. 🧱 Gradualization

Build belief before making bold claims. Start with what the reader already accepts as true, then logically lead to your promise.

Example: “Have you always dreamed of speaking another language?” → Show evidence → “Our course makes you fluent in 3 months.”

Use: logical connectors, questions, rhythm, soft-to-strong progression.



4. 🔄 Redefinition

Remove objections by changing how the product is perceived.

* Simplify: Turn complexity into ease.
* Ex: TV repair = “3 simple steps anyone can follow.”
* Escalate: Tie to a higher goal.
* Ex: Fluent English = Career success, not just school skill.
* Reframe price: Emphasize value, not cost.
* Ex: “Costs less than a cup of coffee a day.”


5. ⚙️ Mechanization

Explain how your product actually works.

Example: “This cleaning spray uses enzymes to dissolve grease on contact. Here’s how each stage works…”

This builds trust and perceived value—especially for complex or technical products.



6. 🪞 Concentration

Highlight your competitors’ flaws—and contrast your strengths.

Example: “Old spark plugs need replacing. Our fire injectors self-clean and last 3x longer.”

Show how your product is superior and problem-free.



7. 🕵️ Camouflage

Blend into the editorial style of where your ad appears.

Example: In a medical journal, write formally and cite data. In lifestyle blogs, be conversational and image-rich.

Borrow credibility by matching the reader’s expectations.



PART 3: FINISHING TOUCHES THAT BOOST RESPONSE

🌟 First 50 Words

Grab attention immediately with:

* A bold claim: “1 in 3 adults are sleep-deprived.”
* A big promise: “What if you could retire 10 years early?”
* Reader focus: “Are you tired of feeling tired?”
* Curiosity: “The ancient secret to glowing skin…”



✂️ Right-Length Copy

Let the message + medium determine length:

* Long: For big-ticket, high-trust offers (investment services)
* Medium: Emails, landing pages (mattresses, supplements)
* Short: Social ads, impulse buys (“New Spicy Lime Chips!”)



📌 Subheads = Navigation

Subheads help skimmers follow the story.

Examples:

* “The Problem: Sleepless Nights”
* “The Solution: Deep Rest Without Pills”
* “Proof: What 1,400 Customers Say”



🗣️ Tone = Human

Speak like a friend. Avoid jargon, fluff, or hype.

✅ “Let’s talk about something that’s bugging you...”

❌ “This revolutionary innovation optimizes human efficiency.”


🧮 Momentum

Keep readers moving down the page using bridging phrases.

Momentum Phrases:

* “Let me explain...”
* “Here’s how it works...”
* “But that’s not all...”
* “And there’s more…”
* “So what does that mean for you?”

Use these to maintain flow and curiosity.



😌 Mood

Infuse your copy with the right emotional energy—drama, calm, humor, excitement, urgency—depending on what your reader needs to feel.

Example moods:

* Excitement: “You’ve never experienced anything like this.”
* Urgency: “This offer won’t last.”
* Reassurance: “Thousands have made the switch safely.”



🎯 Sensitivity

Be aware of when to switch tones, angles, or formats.

If your reader is:

* Getting bored? → Add curiosity or switch to storytelling.
* Overwhelmed? → Simplify and recap.
* Skeptical? → Add proof or a testimonial.


✅ Proof, Proof, Proof

Build credibility:

* Stats: “Clinically tested to reduce pain by 68%.”
* Testimonials: “I finally sleep through the night!”
* Demonstrations, expert endorsements, screenshots

📌 Place proof early to build trust, and again after each big claim to reinforce belief.


🧲 Make the Offer Irresistible

* % off + bonus: “Buy now, get 25% off + free guide”
* Free trial: “Try it for 14 days. Cancel anytime.”
* Urgency: “Only 72 left in stock!”



🎯 Strong Close

Reinforce the offer. Create urgency. Be clear.

Examples:

* “Don’t miss out. Offer ends tonight.”
* “Click below to get started risk-free.”



🔚 FINAL WORD

Breakthrough copy is about understanding people—what they want, what they fear, and what they dream of.

Write daily. Study what works. Analyze every ad. Keep chasing the big idea.
Great Leads: A Manual for Marketers and Copywriters

This manual summarizes the key strategies and principles outlined in "Great Leads: The Six Easiest Ways to Start Any Sales Message" by Michael Masterson and John Forde. It aims to guide novice marketers and copywriters in crafting compelling leads that grab attention and drive results.

Introduction: The Power of the Lead

The lead, the beginning of your sales copy, is the most crucial part of your advertisement. It's your first and only chance to emotionally persuade your prospect. If you fail to capture their interest in the lead, you'll likely lose them, regardless of how strong the rest of your copy is. Experienced copywriters recognize the lead's critical role. By focusing on crafting powerful leads, you can significantly improve the performance of your promotions.

The Rule of One: One Big Idea

A fundamental strategy for direct response success is the Rule of One: focus on one big idea in your lead. Readers are generally looking for a single, useful suggestion or idea. Promotions built around a single, powerful core idea are more likely to jolt the consumer out of indifference, make them remember your advertising, and take action.

Key Aspects of the Rule of One:

* One Good Idea: Present one central, compelling idea. For example, the idea that "There’s no product easier to create or sell online than a simple, straightforward instructional or how-to e-book".
* One Core Emotion: Aim to evoke a single, strong emotion that compels the reader to respond. For the e-book example, the emotion is "It is simple! I bet I can do it!".
* One Captivating Story or Compelling Fact: Support your idea with a brief, engaging story or a powerful, relevant fact. For instance, the 17-word story: "Would you be interested in investing $175 to make $20,727? That’s exactly what Bob Bly just accomplished!".
* One Single, Desirable Benefit: Clearly highlight the primary benefit the reader will gain. In the e-book example: "Now, I want to show you how to make huge profits creating and selling simple e-books…".
* One Inevitable Response: Guide the reader towards a single, clear call to action. For example, "click here now" to get the book for a special price.

Sticking to one core idea prevents your message from becoming diluted and allows for a stronger emotional impact. Avoid the "tossed salad" approach of throwing in multiple features and benefits without a central focus.

The Simple Question: Understanding Customer Awareness

To craft effective leads, ask yourself: What does your customer already know?. Understanding your prospect's level of awareness about their problem, potential solutions, and your product is crucial in determining the most effective lead type. This concept was first highlighted by copywriting legend Gene Schwartz.

Gene Schwartz's Five Levels of Customer Awareness:

1. The Most Aware: Your prospect knows your product and just needs to know the deal.
  * Example: Loyal customers of a specific brand reacting to a new product announcement or a special offer.
2. Product-Aware: Your prospect knows about your product but isn't fully convinced they need it or that you're the best option.
  * Example: Someone who has researched different types of tax software and is now looking for the best offer and most trustworthy provider.
3. Solution-Aware: Your prospect knows the result they want but isn't aware that your product provides that specific solution.
  * Example: Someone looking to improve their golf swing but doesn't know about a specific training program you offer.
4. Problem-Aware: Your prospect knows they have a problem but isn't aware of any specific solutions.
  * Example: Someone experiencing constant fatigue who hasn't yet explored potential causes or remedies.
5. Completely Unaware: Your prospect doesn't know who you are, your product, or that they even have a specific problem worth solving.
  * Example: Introducing a completely new and innovative technology to a market that isn't yet aware of the underlying need.

Knowing your audience's awareness level will guide you in choosing the most appropriate type of lead and the directness of your approach.

Direct or Indirect?

Your lead can take a direct or indirect approach.

* Direct Leads: These get straight to the point, often mentioning the product or the offer immediately. They work best with more aware and already engaged customers.
  * Example Headlines: "Don't Pay a Penny For This Book Until it Doubles Your Power to Learn", "Why We’re Giving You 12 FREE Months of Alexander Green’s Momentum Alert".
* Indirect Leads: These avoid direct claims or references to the product in the initial stages, aiming to draw the reader in through a story, secret, or startling statement. They are often more effective with less aware or skeptical customers.
  * Example Lead: The "tale of two young men" opening for The Wall Street Journal, which doesn't mention the newspaper or a subscription offer initially.

The level of your customer's awareness plays a significant role in deciding whether a direct or indirect approach will be more effective.

The Six Archetypal Lead Types

The book identifies six major categories of direct and indirect leads.

1. The Offer Lead (Direct)

This type of lead immediately focuses on the deal. It's most effective with highly aware customers who are ready to buy.

Key Elements of an Offer Lead:

* Focus on the most emotionally-compelling detail of your offer.
* Underscore the most valuable benefit of that deal.
* Elaborate on the deal-benefit in the lead.
* Include a compelling "reason why" you're making the offer. This helps address skepticism.

Examples:

* Headline: "Guaranteed To Improve Your Child’s School Marks — or you pay nothing! The Fabulous New Teaching Machine Automated Speed Learning Method". This immediately presents a strong guarantee and the product.
* Headline: "May I send you 3 FREE Issues of what may be the most influential (as well as selectively distributed) newsweekly in the world?". This directly offers a free trial.

2. The Promise Lead (Direct)

This lead clearly states or implies a significant benefit the customer will receive. It works best with "mostly aware" prospects who are almost ready to buy.

Key Aspects of a Promise Lead:

* Focus on what the product will do for the customer, emphasizing the emotional benefits.
* Ensure the promise is believable and can be proven. Use testimonials, data, etc., for credibility.
* Understand what has been promised before in your market to differentiate your promise.

Examples:

* Headline: "How to Win Friends and Influence People". This title itself is a powerful promise of social success.
* Lead (for an advertising course): "It pays to promise a benefit which is unique and competitive, and the product must deliver the benefit you promise. Most advertising promises nothing. It is doomed to fail in the marketplace ... Headlines that promise to benefit sell more than those that don’t.". This directly promises a valuable skill.

3. The Problem-Solution Lead (Direct to Indirect)

This lead identifies a key problem or worry of the target audience and then presents the product as the solution. It works well with prospects who are aware of their problem.

Key Elements of a Problem-Solution Lead:

* Immediately address a significant pain point or frustration.
* Show empathy and understanding of the prospect's problem.
* Position your product or service as the direct and effective solution.

Examples:

* Headline: "If you’re feeling run down, then this will pick you up." (For Geritol). This classic "If-then" approach directly links the problem (feeling run down) with the solution (the product).
* Lead (for advertising advice): Starts by listing "Seven Costly Mistakes Most Advertisers Make". This immediately grabs the attention of advertisers who want to avoid these mistakes.

4. The Secret Lead (Indirect)

This lead teases the prospect with a hard-to-come-by piece of knowledge, formula, or "secret" that will provide a significant benefit. It works well across various awareness levels but is particularly effective at sparking curiosity.

Key Rules for Writing Secret Leads:

* The secret must be intriguing and beneficial.
* The secret should connect to a major benefit of the product.
* The secret is not disclosed in the lead.
* As the letter progresses, more clues are given, building intrigue without revealing the full secret.
* Create the secret by finding something unique about the product or by neologizing/transubstantiating a known benefit (giving it a new, intriguing name).

Examples:

* Headline: "Closed to New Investors for the Last 6 Years — Now Open Again ... The ‘Chaffee Royalty Program’ That Turned Every $1 Into $50". The invented name and the promise create intrigue.
* Headline: "The Only Investment Legally Obligated to Pay You 181% Gains By June 15, 2009 It’s not a stock, option, or gov’t bond ... But this unusual investment has crushed the S&P 500 by ...". This teases an unusual and high-return investment.

5. The Proclamation Lead (Indirect)

This lead aims to jar the "unaware" reader with a surprising, shocking, or bold statement, factoid, or prediction. The goal is to disarm skepticism and create curiosity.

Key Principles of a Proclamation Lead:

* It must be remarkable and unexpected to grab attention.
* It must be relevant to the prospect's interests or desires.
* It must ultimately lead to a relevant claim or promise.
* The product's USP is usually revealed after proving the proclamation.
* Return to the theme of the proclamation at the close of your copy and in your guarantee.

Examples:

* Cover Title: "Read This Or Die" (for a health product). This shocking statement immediately demands attention.
* Headline: "Warning: Your 1995 PDR is Obsolete" (for doctors). This bold statement targets a specific audience with a potentially concerning fact.
* Title Copy: "Why Most 20th Century Medicine Will Soon Become Extinct". This surprising prediction captures attention.

6. The Story Lead (Indirect)

This lead opens with an engaging story designed to connect with the reader emotionally. It's a powerful way to engage readers who may be skeptical or unfamiliar with the product.

Key Aspects of a Story Lead:

* The story should feature a protagonist the reader can identify with.
* It should present a conflict or situation that evokes emotion.
* The story should ultimately lead to the product or service as the solution or key takeaway.
* Ensure the story is concise and relevant to the core idea of the promotion.
* The story should contain an implied promise.

Examples:

* The opening of the $2 Billion Sales Letter, which tells a relatable story about the author's experience in a library facing a deadline. This builds curiosity and implies a lesson to be learned.
* Headline Structure (inspired by a classic piano course ad): "They laughed when I sat down at the computer — But when I started to use it!". This short narrative creates intrigue and implies a surprising transformation.

Making the System Work for You

By understanding these six lead archetypes and the principles behind them, you can approach your copywriting with greater confidence. Consider your target audience's awareness level and choose the lead type that best suits their needs and knowledge. Remember to focus on one big idea and craft your lead to emotionally engage your reader. Test different approaches when possible to see what resonates best with your specific audience and product.

Conclusion

Mastering the art of writing compelling leads is fundamental to direct response success. By applying the strategies and principles outlined in this manual, you can significantly enhance your ability to grab your prospect's attention, build their interest, and ultimately drive them to take action. Remember to always keep your audience in mind and strive for clarity, conciseness, and emotional connection in your leads.
How to Write Bullets Like Bencivenga: 8 Rules and Powerful Examples

This manual provides instructions on how to write effective bullet points, inspired by the techniques of successful copywriters like Gary Bencivenga.

The Purpose of Bullets

Bullets serve several key purposes in your copy:

* Give the eyes a rest.
* Demand attention.
* Tease with desirable benefits.
* Weaken the reader’s willpower.

People are often resistant to sales messages. Your job is to lower their defenses, and compelling bullets can make spending seem involuntary.

Remember. A bullet is a mini-headline. So whatever formula you subscribe to for writing headlines, use that also for bullets. The only difference between the two is this:

Bullets… unlike their needy, older brother… go inside the copy. And they come in armies… Directing the reader’s desire towards a common CTA.

You only get 1 headline per CTA. But you get unlimited bullets.

After a good bullet dump, your reader should be giddy about buying whatever offer you have at the bottom of the page.

The 8 Rules to Writing Whiplash-Inflicting Bullets

Think of a bullet point as a mini-headline. Apply your headline-writing formulas to your bullets. Unlike headlines, you can use multiple bullets to direct the reader's desire toward a single call to action (CTA).

Here are the general rules:

1. Promise a benefit: This should be linked directly to the impending CTA. It needs to get the reader excited and curious.

2. Make them easy on the eyes: One of the many purposes of a bullet list is to give the eyes a rest. BUT, you should make it even easier on the eyes by structuring your bullets uniformly. Make each the same length as the one prior.

3. Keep it simple: Avoid sub-bullets and such. They should be clear, concise and awesome.

4. Order them according to grammar and word usage: Don’t alternate between how to’s, what’s, discover’s, learn’s, etc. If you’ve got some how to’s and some learn’s, section them accordingly. Don’t mix them.

5. Alternate between emboldened and unemboldened: Iit’s another great way to keep the reader’s eyes from wandering.  Consider writing every other bullet in bold type.

6. Curiosity is key: Every bullet should open a loop. The only closure? Your CTA. Don’t write sentences. Write “mini-headlines.” The is, perhaps, the #1 rule to writing killer bullets. “Write bullets that wound, with the only remedy coming from buying the product” – Ken McCarthy.

7. The dump: There is no limit to how many bullets you can include in the dump. The more the merrier. Don’t be repetitive, but be liberal with it. As the reader slides down your list, their curiosity should only become more intense. Take advantage of that by pouring it on.

8. Fantasy: Most people browse the internet alone. No one’s around to judge them… Exploit this by triggering mankind’s darkest fantasies: envy, laziness, lust, greed, gluttony, power, control, etc.

To maximize the impact of your bullets, activate the reader's "salivary glands" before presenting them. Lead into your bullet list with a question that engages the reader.

  * [Insert clear order instructions.]

Copywriting Manual: Message-Market Match

This manual outlines a strategy for aligning your marketing message with your market's awareness level to maximize effectiveness. It combines the Eugene Schwartz Market Awareness Model with the PAISA copywriting formula.

Section 1: The Eugene Schwartz Market Awareness Model

The Eugene Schwartz Market Awareness Model describes the stages a prospect goes through before making a purchase:

* Unaware: The prospect is not aware of having a problem or a need.
* Problem Aware: The prospect recognizes they have a problem.
* Solution Aware: The prospect is aware that solutions to their problem exist.
* You Aware: The prospect becomes aware of specific brands, products, or services that offer solutions.
* Deal Ready: The prospect is ready to make a purchase.

Section 2: The PAISA Copywriting Formula

The PAISA formula is a structure for crafting marketing messages:

* Problem: Define the problem or unfulfilled need that the prospect is experiencing.
* Agitate: Emphasize the negative emotional experience of the problem.
* Invalidate: Explain why other potential solutions are not ideal.
* Solution: Define the ideal solution and align it with your specific offering.
* Ask: Make a specific offer for your product or service.

Section 3: The Golden Key: Message-Market Match

The core concept is to align your marketing message (PAISA) with the prospect's current stage of awareness (Eugene Schwartz Model).

* Unaware Market:
  * Start by defining the problem.
  * Agitate the problem to make the prospect aware of the need for a solution.
  * Introduce the concept of a solution.
  * Invalidate other potential solutions.
  * Present your solution as the ideal option.
  * Make an offer.
* Problem Aware Market:
  * Acknowledge the problem.
  * Agitate the problem to emphasize the need for urgency in finding a solution.
  * Compare and invalidate other potential solutions.
  * Define the ideal solution.
  * Make an offer.
* Solution Aware Market:
  * Focus on comparing solutions.
  * Invalidate competitor solutions.
  * Present your solution as the superior choice.
  * Make an offer.
* Deal Ready Market:
  * Focus on your product and its benefits.
  * Make a compelling offer.

Section 4: Application

* Segmentation: Recognize that your audience will be in different stages of awareness.
* Tailoring Messages: Craft different marketing messages to address each segment effectively.
* Retargeting: When retargeting, focus on prospects who are further along in the awareness journey (solution aware or deal-ready).
* Long-Term Communication: Even with a warm audience, it can be beneficial to reintroduce earlier stages of the PAISA formula to reinforce your message.

By understanding your audience's awareness level and applying the PAISA formula accordingly, you can create more effective and persuasive marketing messages.
